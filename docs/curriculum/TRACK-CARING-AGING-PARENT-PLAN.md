## 1. Course philosophy

This course is for the person carrying the weight: the one who answers the late-night phone call, who books the specialist, who sits in the assessment, who watches a parent disappear in pieces. It is also for the person who lives across an ocean and feels the guilt of distance, and the person whose parent now lives in their living room and feels the guilt of proximity. It is for the daughter of the mother she didn't get along with and now bathes. It is for the son whose father no longer recognises him.

It is not a how-to. It does not tell you which care plan to buy or whose name to put on a power of attorney. Those decisions belong to you, your parent, and a professional team — a doctor, a social worker, a lawyer, an aged-care assessor in your jurisdiction. We will say this often. The course works on the layer underneath those decisions: what is happening to *you*, what you can name, and what you can carry without breaking.

The course is anchored in evidence-based and practitioner-tested traditions — Pauline Boss on ambiguous loss, Atul Gawande's *Being Mortal*, adult attachment theory (Bowlby/Ainsworth → Levine & Heller), Brené Brown on shame and vulnerability, Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (Hayes), and George Bonanno's modern grief science. The dignity-based self-kindness frame ([CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md §6](./CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md)) replaces Kristin Neff's earlier framework where the course addresses caregiver shame. Where these traditions disagree, we say so. Where they have been corrected by later research (Kübler-Ross stages, for instance), we teach the corrected science.

We assume you may be tired, interrupted, and reading on a phone in a hospital corridor. Lessons are short. Missions are small. Every lesson has an exit ramp.

## 2. The unit

Ten lessons, one unit, sequenced from naming what is happening (Lessons 1–3), through the relational mess (4–6), into the hardest conversations (7–8), and out toward grief and self-preservation (9–10). Earlier lessons do not depend on later ones, but Lessons 7–10 assume the user has the language introduced in 1–6.

### Unit 1 — Caring for an Aging Parent
Emoji: `🤝`
Learning arc: From "I am drowning and I don't have words for this" to "I have language, frames, and small practices for the hardest part of adult life."
Anchor traditions: Pauline Boss (ambiguous loss), Atul Gawande (*Being Mortal*), adult attachment theory, Brené Brown on shame/vulnerability, ACT (Hayes), Bonanno (modern grief science), the dignity-based self-kindness frame ([CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md §6](./CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md)).
Why one unit: Caregiving is one continuous experience, not ten domains. The arc is more important than the categories.

## 4. Pedagogical decisions

These are the explicit choices that distinguish this track from generic caregiver self-help.

1. **No medical, legal, or financial advice. Ever.**
   The course is repeatedly explicit: those decisions are for the user, their parent, and their professional team. We name where the user should bring in a doctor, social worker, lawyer, geriatrician, or aged-care assessor. We do not tell them what to choose. This is a content-design choice, not just a disclaimer — it shapes every mission. Missions are about *the user's interior*, not about scheduling a power-of-attorney appointment.

2. **Cultural humility is load-bearing, not decorative.**
   Caregiving norms vary enormously. We write for a daughter in Manila where multi-generational households are the default; for a son in Sydney whose mother is in residential care and he visits twice a week; for a grandchild in rural Zimbabwe who *is* the care system. Every dialogue includes worked examples that span at least two of these realities. We name filial piety where relevant (Confucian, African ubuntu, Filipino *utang na loob*) without flattening or moralising about it.

3. **Pauline Boss on ambiguous loss is the spine of this track.**
   Boss's central claim — that we grieve people who are physically present but psychologically gone (and vice versa for those far away) — is the single most clarifying frame in caregiver psychology. Lesson 2 introduces it explicitly. Lessons 3, 6, 9, and 10 return to it. Many caregivers have never been given a name for what they are feeling. This track gives them one.

4. **Anticipatory grief is taught as legitimate grief, not as "pre-grief" or rehearsal.**
   Anchored in Bonanno's modern grief science, which corrects the Kübler-Ross stages: most people do not move through five tidy stages, most are more resilient than predicted, and grief that begins before death is real grief — not a phase, not a warning, not preparation. Lesson 3 makes this claim and Lesson 10 returns to it after the death.

5. **Adult attachment theory is taught as "your childhood wiring reactivates" — not as a personality test.**
   When a parent becomes dependent, the user's attachment system, formed with that same parent decades earlier, comes back online. We teach this in Lesson 4 in concrete somatic-behavioural terms (the way you become 14 again in their kitchen) rather than as a four-quadrant style classification. Anchored in Bowlby/Ainsworth → Mary Main (Adult Attachment Interview) → modern relational neurobiology (Siegel).

6. **Kindness to yourself is the antidote to caregiver shame, not a luxury.**
   The clinical data converges across compassion-focused therapy (Gilbert), shame-resilience research (Brown), and caregiver outcome studies: self-criticism predicts caregiver burnout, depression, and worse outcomes for the parent. Lessons 6, 9, and 10 use the dignity-based three-move framework — **honest acknowledgement** (truth-telling about what you feel, including the cruel and resentful moments), **shared dignity** (you are not uniquely failing; every caregiver hits these moments, and it doesn't make any of you less worth caring for), and **kindness to yourself** (the same charity you would extend to a friend) — especially around the days you have snapped, resented, or wished it would end. Full framework in [CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md §6](./CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md).

7. **ACT for living with what you cannot fix.**
   Caregiving is a domain in which problem-solving fails. Many things cannot be fixed — the dementia is not curable, the sibling is not coming, the parent will die. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (Hayes) is the most honest tradition we have for this: clean pain vs dirty pain, values-aligned action in the presence of unfixable circumstance. Lessons 6, 8, 9, and 10 lean on it.

8. **Bonanno's resilience research, not the popular grief myths.**
   Most caregivers are far more resilient than they believe in the dark moments. Bonanno's longitudinal data shows that resilience is the modal outcome of bereavement — even after long, hard caregiving. We teach this in Lessons 3 and 10 as a counter to the "you'll never recover" narrative, while explicitly making room for the smaller proportion who have a harder course (complicated grief is real and we name it).

9. **Identity-first framing.**
   Per the methodology: each lesson opens with an identity move. "Today's the day you become someone who can be present without being depleted." "Today's the day you stop trying to fix what cannot be fixed and start being there for it." We do not infantilise.

10. **Every lesson has an exit ramp.**
    Caregiving content can detonate at unpredictable times. Every Phase 8 conversation includes an explicit "you can stop here" move. Every mission is small enough to be done in 30 seconds, in a hospital corridor, at 3am. No mission is "have the hard conversation today" — we teach the *preparation* for hard conversations and let the user choose the timing.

11. **Worked examples include the hard ones — by policy.**
    Every lesson contains at least one worked example that does not have a tidy outcome: the parent whose dementia has erased who they were, the sibling who refuses to help, the end-of-life decision made by a family at war, the parent the user did not get along with who now needs them. Sentimental caregiver content skips these. We include them because pretending they don't exist makes users with those situations feel even more alone.

## 7. Critical review

### Truth — are claims evidence-grounded?

- Pauline Boss's *ambiguous loss* framework is well-established in family-therapy literature; cited explicitly.
- Bonanno's resilience research has corrected Kübler-Ross's five stages; we teach the corrected science (Lessons 3 and 10).
- Adult attachment theory's clinical utility is well-attested. The four-style typology is more contested than the mainstream presentation suggests; we teach the *mechanism* (childhood patterns reactivating) rather than asking the user to self-identify a quadrant.
- The clinical evidence that self-criticism predicts worse caregiver outcomes — and that warmth toward oneself improves them — is robust and converges across multiple research traditions (compassion-focused therapy, shame resilience, caregiver burnout studies).
- Gawande's *Being Mortal* is reportage, not research, but it is grounded in serious geriatric and palliative-care literature; we cite it as a synthesising work, not as primary evidence.

Where I deliberately did not say "studies show":
- Sibling caregiving dynamics. The research literature here is sparse and culturally narrow. Lesson 5 is grounded in family-systems practice and clinician-experience, not in epidemiology, and the lesson says so.
- "Personality shift" in dementia. Real and clinically documented, but the trajectory varies enormously by aetiology (Alzheimer's vs vascular vs frontotemporal vs Lewy body). We do not teach trajectory; we teach the user's relationship to the shift.

### Cultural sensitivity

Every dialogue contains worked examples for at least two of: a Manila multi-generational household, a Sydney resident-care facility, a rural Zimbabwean family who *is* the care system. We avoid Western nuclear-family defaults. We name filial piety, ubuntu, and *utang na loob* without moralising. Where institutional care is the user's reality, it is treated with the same respect as multi-generational care — neither is presented as more virtuous.

### Pedagogy — does the 7-phase Rwendo Method work?

Yes, with the same caveats noted in the Knowing Yourself plan: `build_sentence` is sometimes forced (kept where the "sentence" is a memorable principle), `multiple_choice` is genuinely diagnostic for caregiver content (used 1× per lesson, not 2×), and Phase 8 is the most important component because users will arrive at these lessons in real distress and need a real conversation, not a worksheet.

### Quality bar

Should sit alongside *Being Mortal* (Gawande), *When the Time Comes* (Span), and *Loving Someone Who Has Dementia* (Boss). Honest, generous, never sanitised. The litmus test for each lesson: would a reader who is the only sibling caring for a parent with frontotemporal dementia in a country where home-care doesn't exist feel seen, or feel that their reality has been written out?
