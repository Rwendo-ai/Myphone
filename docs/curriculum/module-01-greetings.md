# Module 1: Greetings & Respect (Tsika)

> "Before you can say anything in Shona, you must say hello — and in Shona, hello means *I see you, I respect you, I care how you are.*"

This module is the foundation of everything that follows. By the end, the learner can navigate any first encounter in Shona with cultural confidence.

## What the Learner Walks Away With

By the end of Module 1, a learner can:
- Greet appropriately at any time of day (morning, afternoon, evening)
- Distinguish respectful (plural) vs. informal (singular) forms
- Respond fluently to common greetings without thinking
- Say goodbye in two ways
- Understand *why* greetings matter culturally — not just the words

## The 10 Lessons

### Lesson 1: Mangwanani — Good Morning *(detailed in lesson-template.md)*
**New chunks:** Mangwanani · Mamuka sei? · Ndamuka, kana mamukawo
**Pattern:** Person markers — Ma- (you respectful), W- (you informal), Nd- (I)
**Cultural hook:** Greetings are wellness checks, not "hi"
**Mission:** Greet someone with Mangwanani today

---

### Lesson 2: Wamuka Sei? — Greeting a Friend
**New chunks:** Wamuka sei? · Ndamuka, kana wamukawo · Hesi
**Pattern:** Switch to informal — W- prefix for friends/peers
**Cultural hook:** Friends and family use the singular form. Using respectful form with a close friend can feel cold.
**Exercise highlight:** Side-by-side compare — Mamuka sei? vs Wamuka sei?
**Rwen dialogue:** Rwen meets the user as a friend — uses Wamuka sei?
**Mission:** Greet a sibling, friend, or partner informally today

---

### Lesson 3: Maswera Sei? — Afternoon Greetings
**New chunks:** Maswera sei? · Ndaswera, kana maswerawo · Masikati
**Pattern:** Spending the day vs. waking the day — *swera* root means "spent the day"
**Cultural hook:** Time of day matters — using *Mangwanani* in the afternoon is awkward, like saying "good morning" at 4pm in English.
**Exercise highlight:** Choose the right greeting based on time-of-day prompt
**Rwen dialogue:** The afternoon market — greet the vendor
**Mission:** Use Maswera sei? in conversation today

---

### Lesson 4: Manheru — Good Evening
**New chunks:** Manheru · Manheru akanaka · Hona
**Pattern:** Evening forms — fewer plural/informal distinctions
**Cultural hook:** *Manheru akanaka* (good evening) carries warmth — used when settling in, sharing food
**Exercise highlight:** Match all four greetings (morning/afternoon/evening/friend) to scenarios
**Rwen dialogue:** Family gathers in the evening — Rwen greets each person
**Mission:** Say Manheru akanaka to someone before bed tonight

---

### Lesson 5: Makadii — The Universal Greeting
**New chunks:** Makadii · Ndiripo, makadiiwo · Wakadii?
**Pattern:** *Makadii* works any time of day — the safety net
**Cultural hook:** When in doubt, *Makadii* is always correct. Used between strangers and across cultures.
**Exercise highlight:** Convert any time-specific greeting to *Makadii*
**Rwen dialogue:** Two travellers meet on a road — neither knows the other
**Mission:** Greet a stranger with Makadii today

---

### Lesson 6: Zita Rako Ndiani? — What's Your Name?
**New chunks:** Zita rako ndiani? · Zita rangu ndi[name] · Wakanaka kukuziva
**Pattern:** Possessives — *rako* (yours), *rangu* (mine)
**Cultural hook:** Names carry meaning in Shona — many names are sentences. Asking is a sign of interest.
**Exercise highlight:** Build the sentence "My name is [your real name]"
**Rwen dialogue:** Rwen asks the user's name (using their real name from profile)
**Mission:** Introduce yourself in Shona to one person today

---

### Lesson 7: Wabva Kupi? — Where Are You From?
**New chunks:** Wabva kupi? · Ndabva ku[place] · Ndinogara ku[place]
**Pattern:** *Ku-* prefix marks a place — locative form
**Cultural hook:** "Where are you from?" is bonding, not interrogation. Origin matters.
**Exercise highlight:** Translate origin sentences using user's home country
**Rwen dialogue:** Rwen asks where the user is from — adapts to their answer
**Mission:** Tell someone where you live in Shona today

---

### Lesson 8: Mese — When You Are Many
**New chunks:** Makadii mese · Tiripo · Tisangane
**Pattern:** Greeting groups — *mese* = "all of you"
**Cultural hook:** Group greetings are a single act in Shona — you greet the whole room before settling in
**Exercise highlight:** Multiple choice — which greeting fits a room of people?
**Rwen dialogue:** Rwen enters a family gathering — greets everyone
**Mission:** Greet a group with *Makadii mese* today

---

### Lesson 9: Goodbye — Sara Zvakanaka
**New chunks:** Sara zvakanaka · Famba zvakanaka · Tichaonana
**Pattern:** *Sara* (stay) vs *famba* (go) — the leaver says one, the stayer says the other
**Cultural hook:** Saying goodbye in Shona blesses the other's path. *Famba zvakanaka* literally means "go well".
**Exercise highlight:** Decide which farewell — are you leaving or staying?
**Rwen dialogue:** End-of-day scene — Rwen and user part ways
**Mission:** Say *Famba zvakanaka* to someone leaving today

---

### Lesson 10: The Greeting Conversation — Putting It All Together
**No new chunks** — this is interleaved review and integration
**Pattern:** Full greeting flow — greet → name exchange → origin → goodbye
**Cultural hook:** A complete first encounter in Shona, start to finish
**Exercise highlight:** Long-form dialogue choose-your-own-words with Rwen
**Rwen dialogue:** Full 10-line conversation — user navigates with multiple-choice responses
**Mission:** Have a complete Shona introduction with someone today (or in the mirror)
**Module reward unlocked:** "Tsika Initiate" badge + Rwen's grateful celebration
