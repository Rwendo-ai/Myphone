## 1. Track philosophy

This is a course for people in the dip. After a public failure, a job loss, a divorce, a creative project that flopped, a diagnosis that ended an old life, parenting a child who now looks down on you, ageing into invisibility, becoming a beginner again at sixty after being expert at forty. The goal is not to make the user feel better quickly. The goal is to give them a small, honest set of moves for rebuilding self-trust on a timescale measured in weeks and months — not a weekend retreat.

The course refuses three common moves of the genre. It does not rebrand failure as a "gift" or a "lesson in disguise" — sometimes failure is just failure, and the work is to live well alongside the loss rather than to redeem it. It does not promise that the imposter feeling goes away — for most people it never quite does, and the skill is acting anyway. It does not collapse Carol Dweck's growth mindset into a bumper sticker — Dweck herself has spent years correcting the popular version, and we honour that correction.

What the course does instead: it teaches the user to tell the truth about what happened (without catastrophising or minimising), to separate the failure from the self, to gather small evidence that rebuilds, to clean up comparison-mind, to ask for help when they used to be the one giving it, to be a beginner with grace, and to sit with the fact that the imposter feeling will sit with them too.

It is identity-first. Today's the day you become someone who can be a beginner without flinching.

## 2. The 8 lessons

The sequence is intentional. Lessons 1–3 work on the inner frame: telling the truth, separating self from outcome, breaking down "I'm a failure" into thoughts that can be examined. Lessons 4–5 work on the social field: comparison and asking for help. Lessons 6–8 are the rebuild: small evidence, becoming a beginner, and the long-haul truth that confidence was never the point.

### Lesson 1 — What Actually Happened
Anchor traditions: CBT (Beck, Burns) on the cognitive distortions — catastrophising and minimising; Brené Brown on the difference between shame and guilt; Stoic *premeditatio malorum* applied retrospectively.
Skill: The user writes one paragraph describing what happened in the dip — without catastrophising ("my life is over"), without minimising ("it wasn't that bad"), and without rehearsing the story for the hundredth time. Just what occurred.

### Lesson 2 — You Are Not Your Failure
Anchor traditions: ACT defusion (Hayes) — separating the self from the story; Brené Brown on shame ("I am bad") vs guilt ("I did a bad thing"); IFS-adjacent language for the part that took the hit.
Skill: The user catches one "I am a failure" sentence and rewrites it as a defused observation ("I am noticing the thought that I am a failure, after a thing that did fail").

### Lesson 3 — Catastrophising, Mind-Reading, Fortune-Telling
Anchor traditions: CBT (Beck, Burns) on the specific distortions that flare after a knock; Stoic *premeditatio* corrected — imagining bad outcomes precisely, not vaguely.
Skill: The user names which of the three distortions is most active for them right now and produces one realistic counter-statement that is not artificial positivity.

### Lesson 4 — The Comparison Spiral
Anchor traditions: Festinger's social comparison theory; Kristin Neff on common humanity; Stoic view-from-above (Marcus Aurelius); modern attention-economy critique (Odell, Burkeman).
Skill: The user identifies one comparison loop they fall into (a person, a feed, a peer) and chooses one specific intervention — mute, unfollow, time-cap, or pre-commit to a different signal of progress.

### Lesson 5 — Asking for Help When You're the One Who Used to Give It
Anchor traditions: Kristin Neff on self-compassion as a precondition for change; Brené Brown on vulnerability as the price of connection; attachment research (Holt-Lunstad) on the health cost of isolation.
Skill: The user names one person they have been avoiding telling, and drafts the first three sentences of what they would say.

### Lesson 6 — Small Evidence
Anchor traditions: Bandura on self-efficacy ("mastery experience" as the strongest source); BJ Fogg on tiny habits; CBT behavioural activation; Carol Dweck on the growth mindset *correctly* understood — effort applied with strategy and mentorship, not vague "trying."
Skill: The user identifies one tiny, specific, recently-completed action that counts as evidence of capability, and one tiny, specific action they will take in the next 24 hours.

### Lesson 7 — Becoming a Beginner Again
Anchor traditions: Dweck's growth mindset, with the corrections (it is not "anyone can do anything if they try" — it is the willingness to be bad at things long enough to get less bad); Shunryu Suzuki's *shoshin* (beginner's mind); ACT acceptance of discomfort in service of values.
Skill: The user names one thing they used to be expert at, one thing they are now a beginner at, and writes one sentence that holds both without the second invalidating the first.

### Lesson 8 — The Imposter Feeling That Doesn't Leave
Anchor traditions: Clance & Imes' original "imposter phenomenon" research, with the modern correction (it is widely shared, it does not predict actual incompetence, it is often a feature of competence not a bug); ACT — acting on values in the presence of unwelcome feelings; Stoic acceptance of what cannot be controlled (the feeling itself); Maya Angelou's reported lifelong imposter feeling, kept honestly.
Skill: The user articulates one thing they will keep doing this week even if the imposter feeling shows up — and recognises that the goal is not to feel confident but to act in accordance with their values whether they feel confident or not.

## 3. Pedagogical decisions specific to this track

1. **Failure is not always a gift.** We refuse the LinkedIn rebrand. Some failures cost real things — a marriage, a business, a body that worked, years of a career. The course is for the people for whom the failure was real, not for those repackaging a setback as a Ted talk. Where lessons name worked examples, they include adult losses that did not become triumphs.

2. **Growth mindset is taught with Dweck's own corrections.** The popular version is "anyone can do anything if they try." Dweck has corrected this repeatedly: a growth mindset is the recognition that ability is *developable*, applied with specific effort and strategy and mentorship. It is not an instruction to praise effort regardless of outcome. Lesson 7 teaches the corrected version explicitly.

3. **Self-compassion before self-criticism, always.** Neff's research is unambiguous: self-criticism predicts worse recovery, not better. The course assumes the user is already being hard on themselves and does not add to it. Where the lesson is hard (Lesson 1, Lesson 8), it is hard with warmth.

4. **The imposter feeling is normalised, not eliminated.** The lesson on imposter (8) explicitly teaches that the feeling often persists across an entire career and that the skill is acting alongside it. We do not promise it leaves.

5. **Comparison-mind is treated as hygiene, not a moral failing.** Lesson 4 names comparison as a normal cognitive default, not a sign of weakness. The intervention is environmental (what feeds the eye, who you follow, when you scroll), not exhortation to be better.

6. **Shame vs. guilt is load-bearing.** Drawn from Brown: guilt says "I did a bad thing", shame says "I am a bad thing." Recovery from a knock requires guilt-tier thinking, not shame-tier thinking. This distinction shows up explicitly in Lessons 1, 2, and 5.

7. **Identity-first framing.** Each lesson opens with a sentence about who the user becomes by doing it. "Today's the day you become someone who can…" This is borrowed from James Clear's identity-based habit framing, but applied to recovery rather than acquisition.

8. **Worked examples are adult.** Where examples appear, they are the kinds of failures actual adults face — a startup that died, a marriage that ended badly, getting fired in your fifties, a book that didn't sell, a diagnosis. We avoid student examples where possible.
