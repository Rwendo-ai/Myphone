## 1. Track philosophy

This track is not a parenting course in the prescriptive sense. It does not teach you what kind of parent to be, what your child's behaviour means, or which parenting "style" is correct. It teaches one thing: **how to stay yourself when the pressure of parenting is on**, and how to find your way back when you didn't.

The audience is a parent at any stage — a single mum in Lagos staring down a 3am toddler tantrum, a stay-at-home dad in Sydney whose 14-year-old just slammed a door, a grandparent in Manila raising their grandchild and feeling far too tired for any of this, an adult child standing in their own kitchen wondering why they snapped at the daughter they love. The lessons assume **exhaustion is real**, **mistakes are normal**, and **the parent's nervous system is the regulating organ for the family**. Get that right, and the rest gets workable.

Anchored in: adult attachment under stress (Bowlby/Ainsworth, Levine & Heller's *Attached*), Polyvagal theory (Porges) for nervous-system co-regulation, Daniel Siegel's *The Whole-Brain Child* and *Parenting from the Inside Out*, ACT (Hayes) for values-based parenting under exhaustion, NVC (Rosenberg) for repair, Self-Compassion (Neff) for the parts where you've already screwed up.

Quality target: this track should sit on the shelf next to *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen* (Faber & Mazlish) and *No-Drama Discipline* (Siegel & Bryson). Not Instagram parenting.

## 2. The 12 lessons

The arc moves in four beats: **regulate yourself → repair what's broken → handle the hard hours → live with the long unsolved**. Each lesson is 5–10 minutes and ends with a 5-minute mission tied to a real parenting moment in the next 24 hours.

### Beat 1 — Regulate yourself first (Lessons 1–4)

1. **Your Nervous System Is the Thermostat** — Polyvagal-informed: a child cannot calm down faster than the adult nearest them. The parent regulates first, the child borrows it.
2. **The Three-Second Pause Before You Reply** — Frankl's space, applied to the moment your child is mid-meltdown and asking for a reaction you can't yet give cleanly.
3. **Naming What You Feel Before You Name What They Did** — Siegel's "name it to tame it," upstream into the parent. Anger at a child is almost always carrying something else.
4. **Your Own Attachment Pattern, Showing Up at the Worst Time** — Bowlby/Ainsworth via Levine & Heller. The parent's anxious or avoidant default re-activates under stress and shapes the parenting moment.

### Beat 2 — Repair after the blow-up (Lessons 5–7)

5. **You Will Lose It. What Happens Next Is the Lesson** — Siegel & Bryson's "rupture and repair." The blow-up is not the failure. The failure is the missing repair.
6. **A Real Apology to a Child** — NVC-shaped: what happened, what you felt, what you needed, what you'll try. Without "but" and without making them comfort you.
7. **Self-Compassion for the Parent Who Already Screwed Up** — Neff. Self-criticism predicts worse parenting, not better. The way you talk to yourself after a hard moment shapes the next one.

### Beat 3 — The hard hours (Lessons 8–10)

8. **When Your Child Says "I Hate You"** — Attachment under attack. What the words mean (almost never what they say), what to do with the wound, what not to say back.
9. **The Silent Treatment From a Teen, the Phone in Their Hand** — Avoidant withdrawal in adolescence; the phone as an exit door; co-regulation through proximity rather than confrontation.
10. **Parenting Alone Late at Night When Nothing Is Working** — Single-parent overload, blended-family fatigue, the 2am hour. ACT values work: *what kind of parent do I want to be in this moment, even now*.

### Beat 4 — Live with the long unsolved (Lessons 11–12)

11. **The Child Who Is Hard to Like Today** — Holding love and frustration at the same time without lying about either; co-regulation when warmth doesn't come naturally; the long view.
12. **Good Enough, On Purpose** — Winnicott's "good-enough mother" as a load-bearing frame, not a participation prize. Values-based parenting that survives exhaustion, imperfection, and the long arc.

## 3. Pedagogical decisions

These are explicit choices that distinguish this track from generic parenting content.

1. **The parent's nervous system is the unit of intervention.** Most parenting advice tries to change the child's behaviour. We start with the parent's state. Anchored in Polyvagal theory (Porges) and Siegel's interpersonal neurobiology: an unregulated adult cannot regulate a child, and a regulated adult almost always can. This rules out a large genre of "what to say when your child does X" content as the *first* move.

2. **Repair is treated as more important than prevention.** Siegel & Bryson's clinical position, supported by Gottman on rupture-and-repair: secure attachment is built by ruptured-and-repaired moments more than by perfect ones. We teach the apology directly. We do not pretend you won't lose it.

3. **No idealised wins in worked examples.** Every dialogue includes a parent who lost it, said something cruel, gave in when they said they wouldn't, or didn't have the energy to do the textbook thing. Anchored in the real evidence base: parents who can *recover* from rupture raise more secure children than parents who appear never to rupture (because the latter set is mostly fictional).

4. **No suburban-Western framing.** No "after bath time," no "during the school run in your SUV," no "during your weekly therapy session." The examples assume a small flat, no time, no money, multi-generational households, late shifts, hand-me-downs, and grandparents who live with you or far from you. The skills are universal; the framing must not assume otherwise.

5. **Self-compassion is a tool, not a comfort blanket.** Neff's research is explicit: self-criticism predicts worse behaviour change. Lesson 7 treats self-compassion as an instrument of better parenting tomorrow, not as a sentimental break.

6. **Identity-first language throughout.** "You are the kind of parent who repairs" rather than "good parents repair." Anchored in Clear/Fogg on identity-based behaviour change. The user is not being told what to do — they are being shown who they already are when they show up.

7. **The companion (Rwen) holds the parent, but does not replace human contact.** Where the lesson touches isolated late-night parenting (Lesson 10) or the cruel "I hate you" (Lesson 8), Rwen explicitly directs the user toward real people — a friend, a partner if available, a parents' group — and toward sleep, food, and water. We do not optimise for engagement at the expense of the user's actual support network.

8. **Hard-truth lessons are signposted before they begin.** Lessons 5, 7, 8, and 10 touch raw material (a moment you regret, an "I hate you," parenting at 2am alone). Each opens with a one-line hook that names what's coming, so the user can choose to do it now or later. No ambush.

## 4. Content-care safeguards

Lessons that touch raw nerves and may need an exit ramp:

- **Lesson 5 (Rupture and Repair)** — surfaces a moment the user already regrets. The hook should explicitly say: "If today is too raw, come back to this one tomorrow." The mission is gentle: a single-sentence repair, not a full conversation.
- **Lesson 7 (Self-Compassion for the Parent Who Already Screwed Up)** — high risk of triggering shame spiral, particularly for parents with their own difficult childhood. The Phase 8 dialogue must check whether the user is okay and whether they have a real person to talk to.
- **Lesson 8 ("I Hate You")** — for some parents this overlaps with their own childhood relationship with a parent. The hook flags this. The lesson stays focused on the present moment, not historical analysis (which is therapy work, not lesson work).
- **Lesson 10 (Parenting Alone Late at Night When Nothing Is Working)** — at-risk hours. The lesson signs off with crisis-line guidance from the active jurisdiction pack, normalises calling someone, and explicitly does not push the user to "fix" the night — only to make it through.

For all four lessons, Rwen's Phase 8 dialogue must include a graceful exit option and must avoid escalating prompts that re-traumatise (e.g. "tell me more about the worst moment"). Rwen mirrors and supports; Rwen does not interrogate.
