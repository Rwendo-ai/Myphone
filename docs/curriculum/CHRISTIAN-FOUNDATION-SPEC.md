# Christian-foundation editorial spec

**Scope:** Build Yourself / self-development courses ONLY (Knowing Yourself,
Lost Confidence, Money and Meaning, Caring for an Aging Parent, Parenting
Under Pressure, Hard Conversations at Work, Sleep Repaired, Grief Honestly).
Language courses (Shona, English, French, Tagalog, Chinese) are out of scope —
their cultural notes legitimately reflect regional religion as context.

**Read this brief before authoring any Build Yourself lesson.** It governs
both new lessons and rewrites.

---

## 1. The principle

Rwendo is built by a Christian team. Build Yourself courses must rest on a
Christian foundation — but the customer-facing voice stays neutral. The product
is for everyone, including users who are not Christian and would walk away
from anything that read as evangelism.

The discipline is therefore: **Christian anthropology underneath, neutral
language on top, no competing-religion framing in either layer.**

## 2. Foundation themes (subtly inform every lesson)

These are the values that quietly shape *what we believe a person is* and
*what change is possible for them*. Authors should be able to point to which
foundation theme a lesson rests on without ever naming it in the lesson text.

- **Inherent human dignity** — every person carries inherent worth, prior to
  performance. (Theologically: imago Dei. Operationally: never frame the user
  as broken-and-needs-fixing; frame them as worthy-and-currently-struggling.)
- **Grace over performance** — change does not come from trying harder; it
  comes from being received as you are and growing from there. (Reject
  hustle-culture self-improvement framings.)
- **Hope grounded outside circumstance** — hope is not optimism about
  outcomes; it is a stance available even when the outcome is uncertain.
- **Vocation / being made for purpose** — humans are oriented toward
  something, not just optimising preferences. (Reject pure self-actualisation:
  "find your truth" / "design your life" framings.)
- **Forgiveness and reconciliation** — the path forward is not balancing
  scales (karma), it is releasing what cannot be balanced and choosing
  relationship anyway. (Where reconciliation is impossible, name that
  honestly.)
- **Suffering having meaning** — pain is not an illusion to be escaped or
  a debt to be paid. It can be carried, integrated, and transformed.
- **Love as self-giving** — the highest move is toward another, not toward
  the self. "Self-care" is rest in service of the next act of love, not the
  endpoint.
- **Truth-telling, integrity** — language that matches reality is good in
  itself, regardless of consequences.
- **Accountability inside community** — confession, naming, and being seen
  by another are how change actually happens. Reject pure individualism.

## 3. Surface rules — non-negotiable

- **No scripture quotes, no chapter:verse refs.** Not even paraphrased.
- **No proper names of religious figures.** No Jesus, no Buddha, no Muhammad,
  no Krishna, no Lao Tzu, no Bodhidharma. Saints by their academic names
  (e.g., "Augustine on memory") are borderline — prefer secular framings.
- **No "as a Christian…" / "the Christian view" / "in the steps of…"**
- **No prayer / meditation / chant prescriptions tied to a tradition.**
  ("Take three breaths" is fine; "anapanasati" / "centring prayer" is not.)
- **No karma / balance / energy / chakra / aura vocabulary.**
- **Contemplative pedagogy stays.** Patterns like "between looking and
  seeing", "name what's there", slow attention, observation before reaction
  — these work at the surface. Only the deeper *why* changes.

## 4. Buddhist patterns to remove

These are the specific frames that keep showing up and need to be replaced.

| Buddhist frame | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "Pay attention on purpose, in the present moment, non-judgementally" (Kabat-Zinn) | "Notice what's actually here, before reacting." |
| "The witness" / "witness consciousness" | "Honest seeing" / "stepping back" / "the part of you that can notice" |
| "Mindfulness" as a defined skill | "Attention" or "noticing" |
| Buddhist citations (sati, anapanasati, dukkha, sangha, dharma, koan, sutra, Eightfold, Noble Truths) | Cut entirely. If the cross-tradition list ("every wisdom tradition…") was the structure, replace with a single secular framing. |
| MBSR / Kabat-Zinn / Tara Brach / Shunryū Suzuki / Thich Nhat Hanh citations | Cut. If the *technique* is genuinely useful (e.g., RAIN), describe the technique without the citation. |
| "Two arrows" Buddha metaphor | Cut entirely. The distinction (pain vs. added suffering) is real but doesn't need that metaphor. Reframe: "There's the thing that happened. Then there's everything we add to it. The first is given. The second is ours." |
| Kristin Neff three-pillar self-compassion (mindfulness / common humanity / self-kindness) | **Honest acknowledgement / shared dignity / kindness to self.** Same three moves, Christian anthropology underneath. (See §6.) |
| "Suffering comes from desire / attachment" | "Suffering is real. Some of it is unavoidable. Some of it we add by what we do with it." |
| "Detachment" as virtue | Cut. "Carrying differently" is the replacement (already in Grief Honestly). |
| "Let go" as the goal | "Carry differently" / "release" / "choose to put down what isn't ours to hold" |

## 5. Compatible traditions and citations — allowed

The rule is **not** "Christian-only sources." It is "no foundation incompatible
with Christian anthropology." The following pass the compatibility test and
may be cited (sparingly, by their academic names, without religious framing):

- **Stoicism** — Epictetus on agency, prosoche on attention, "we suffer more
  in imagination than reality." Compatible: agency-over-externals fits
  Christian ethics; doesn't compete with imago-Dei dignity.
- **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)** — values-based action,
  defusion, psychological flexibility. Compatible: clinical, secular,
  non-metaphysical.
- **NVC (Rosenberg)** — observation/feeling/need/request. Compatible:
  language that matches reality + dignity-affirming requests.
- **Evidence-based clinical psychology** — CBT, behavioural activation,
  Pauline Boss on ambiguous loss, Klass et al. on continuing bonds, attachment
  theory, etc. All compatible.
- **Brené Brown** on shame, vulnerability, wholeheartedness — compatible.
- **Habit science** — James Clear, BJ Fogg — compatible.
- **Adam Grant, Daniel Kahneman, secular cognitive science** — compatible.

## 6. Self-compassion replacement framework (cross-cutting)

Kristin Neff's model appears in Lost Confidence, Caring for Aging Parent,
Money and Meaning, and Parenting Under Pressure. Authors writing or rewriting
a self-compassion lesson should use this:

| Neff (Buddhist) | Rwendo (Christian foundation, neutral surface) |
|---|---|
| Mindfulness — non-judgemental awareness of the pain | **Honest acknowledgement** — naming what's actually here, without minimising or dramatising. ("The wound is real. Say what it is.") |
| Common humanity — shared suffering with all beings | **Shared dignity** — you are not uniquely broken. Every person carries inherent worth and inherits the same human limits. ("You are not the only one. And it doesn't make you less worth caring for.") |
| Self-kindness — treating yourself gently | **Kindness to yourself** — extending to yourself the same charity you would extend to a friend. ("You would not say this to someone you love. Don't say it to yourself.") |

The three moves stay the same shape. Only the metaphysics underneath changes:
*shared dignity* (you matter because every person matters) instead of
*shared suffering* (we are all caught in the same illusion).

## 7. Tone — what stays the same

The voice we already have is good and shouldn't change. Specifically:

- **Rwen** stays warm, slow, unhurried, curious, non-shaming. He doesn't preach
  and doesn't rush. (None of that changes; "non-shaming" is *not* the same
  as "non-judgemental awareness" as a defined Buddhist skill.)
- **No chirpy self-help tone.** No "you've got this!" "manifest it!"
  "level up!" framings.
- **Cultural humility stays.** Acknowledging that different cultures handle
  caregiving / grief / family differently is good. Naming Mexican Día de los
  Muertos or Jewish shiva as cultural rituals is fine; naming Buddhist 49-day
  mourning as if it's part of "our" tradition list is not.

## 8. What "rip it out" means concretely

For an existing lesson with Buddhist scaffolding:

1. **Find the deepest assumption** the lesson rests on. (Often: "our problem
   is that we don't pay attention without judgement to the present moment.")
2. **Replace it** with a foundation theme from §2. (E.g., "our problem is
   that we don't see ourselves honestly, and we can't grow from anywhere we
   haven't first honestly named.")
3. **Rewrite the chunks/practice/dialogue** so they teach the new foundation
   without naming it. The structure of the lesson can stay; the content
   inside each chunk often needs replacing.
4. **Remove all citations** to Buddhist teachers, MBSR, Kabat-Zinn, Neff,
   Tara Brach, Suzuki, Thich Nhat Hanh.
5. **Keep compatible citations** (Stoic, ACT, clinical) where they earn their
   place. Don't sprinkle Christian-leaning sources to "balance" — that reads
   as compensating.
6. **Re-read the rewritten lesson** and ask: would a non-Christian user
   reading this feel evangelised? If yes, the surface failed and the layer
   broke. The foundation should be invisible.

## 10. Acceptance check for each rewritten lesson

Before a rewritten lesson is uploaded to Storage, a reviewer should be able
to answer YES to all of the following:

1. No scripture, no religious-figure names, no "as a Christian", no Buddhist
   technical vocabulary, no karma/chakra/energy framings.
2. Every section's underlying claim about *what a person is* maps to one of
   the foundation themes in §2.
3. A non-Christian user reading this lesson cold would not feel evangelised
   in either direction.
4. Where citations remain, every cited tradition or author passes the
   compatibility test in §5.
5. Tone matches the rest of the course — no sudden temperature change.
