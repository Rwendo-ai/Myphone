## 1. Track philosophy

Grief, Honestly is not a recovery program. It is not a five-stage map. It is not a reassurance that everything will be okay. It is a small, careful set of lessons for anyone living inside any kind of grief — death, divorce, estrangement, addiction in someone you love, dementia, identity loss, the loss of a future you imagined, the grief that arrives years after the event.

It is built on three honest claims, each of which corrects a piece of folklore the user has likely been handed by well-meaning people:

1. **Grief does not move in stages.** Kübler-Ross's five stages were derived from interviews with dying patients in 1969 and were never about bereaved people. George Bonanno's modern bereavement research finds that most people do not move through stages at all, and the majority — across cultures, across loss types — show a *resilience trajectory* rather than chronic decline. Grief is messy, recursive, oscillating, and idiosyncratic. We will say so.

2. **Grief does not end. It reshapes you.** The therapeutic promise of "closure" is not supported by the evidence. Continuing-bonds research (Klass, Silverman, Nickman) shows that integrating an ongoing relationship with what was lost is healthier than severing it. We are not in the business of helping users "move on." We are in the business of helping users live alongside what cannot be fixed.

3. **Some grief has no clear ending because the loss has no clear ending.** Pauline Boss's *ambiguous loss* framework names the grief that cannot be closed — the parent with dementia, the child estranged, the partner lost to addiction, the missing person, the refugee's lost homeland. The single most useful gift this track gives a user is this name.

## 2. Anchor traditions

| Tradition / source | What we use it for |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| **George Bonanno** — *The Other Side of Sadness*, *The End of Trauma* | Resilience as the modal trajectory; explicit correction of the stage-model myth; oscillation between grief and life as healthy. |
| **Pauline Boss** — *Ambiguous Loss*, *The Myth of Closure* | The grief of someone alive but lost to you; the grief of unclear endings. Lesson 4. |
| **Megan Devine** — *It's OK That You're Not OK* | Tone, refusal of saccharine reassurance, the line "some things cannot be fixed; they can only be carried." |
| **Joanne Cacciatore** — *Bearing the Unbearable* | Witnessing as the central act; presence over advice; the grief of child loss handled with appropriate seriousness. |
| **ACT** — Hayes, Harris | Living *with* what you cannot change; values-based action while grieving; psychological flexibility instead of "acceptance" as a destination. |
| **Dignity-based self-kindness** ([spec §6](./CHRISTIAN-FOUNDATION-SPEC.md)) | The parts where the user blames themselves — for surviving, for the last argument, for not being there, for the relief. Three moves: honest acknowledgement of what is here; shared dignity (you are not uniquely failing — every griever hits this); kindness toward yourself. Backed by clinical evidence on self-criticism's role in stalling grief recovery (Gilbert; Brown). |
| **Polyvagal theory** — Porges | Grief's somatic load: shutdown, exhaustion, the body grieving even when the mind tries to function. Presented as a useful clinical map, not settled neuroanatomy. |
| **Continuing bonds** — Klass / Silverman / Nickman | The ongoing relationship with what is lost as a sign of integration, not failure. |
| **William Worden** — Tasks of Mourning | Used lightly as a frame (acknowledging the loss; processing the pain; adjusting; finding an enduring connection while moving forward) — without prescribing a sequence. |
| **Cultural traditions** | Día de los Muertos (Mexico), Shiva (Jewish), Kurova guva (Shona), the wake (Irish), tahlil (Indonesian Muslim) — referenced where relevant, never appropriated. Cultural examples are evidence of the universality of *continuing-bond* practices, not endorsements of any tradition's metaphysics. |

## 3. Things this track will NOT say

A short list maintained as a guardrail. None of the following appear in lesson copy:

- "Time heals all wounds." (It doesn't. Time changes the shape of the carrying.)
- "They're in a better place." (We do not make metaphysical claims on the user's behalf.)
- "Everything happens for a reason." (No evidence. Often cruel.)
- "You'll heal." (We say *reshape*, not *heal*.)
- "Stages of grief." (Except when explicitly correcting the myth.)
- "Closure." (Except when explicitly debunking it.)
- "At least…" (Comparative consolation is rarely a kindness.)
- "Be strong." (Strength is not a relevant criterion.)
- "I know how you feel." (You don't. Nobody does.)

## 4. The 10 lessons

The arc moves from *naming* (lessons 1–3) → *naming the harder forms* (4–6) → *the body, the work, the people* (7–8) → *the long view* (9–10). It is not a map to traverse in order, but it is a sequence that supports comprehension.

### Unit 1 — Grief, Honestly

| # | Title | Anchor | Topic |
|---|-------|--------|-------|
| 1 | Grief Is Not a Five-Stage Map | Bonanno; Kübler-Ross corrected | The stage-model myth, why it persists, what the science actually shows. Permission to be wherever you are. |
| 2 | Resilience Is Not Bypassing | Bonanno's resilience trajectory | Most people are resilient. Resilience is not the absence of grief; it is the capacity to grieve and live at the same time. Reject the "I should be more wrecked" trap. |
| 3 | The Loneliness of Grief Other People Don't Understand | Devine; disenfranchised grief (Doka) | The grief that doesn't fit a card. Pet loss, miscarriage, ex-partners, estranged parents, public figures. When others minimise. |
| 4 | When Someone Is Alive But Lost to You — Ambiguous Loss | Pauline Boss | Addiction, dementia, estrangement, missing persons. The grief without a funeral. The single most-used name in this track. |
| 5 | The Loss of a Future You Imagined | ACT; Boss | Divorce that ended badly; infertility; the career that didn't happen; the child who walked away. Grieving a life you never got. |
| 6 | Identity Grief — the Version of You That No Longer Exists | Continuing bonds applied to self; ACT | Illness, career loss, retirement, motherhood, leaving a faith. The you that died alongside the event. |
| 7 | Grief in the Body — Why You Are So Tired | Polyvagal (Porges); Cacciatore | Why grief is exhausting. Sleep, appetite, immune load, the dorsal-vagal shutdown of acute grief. Permission to rest. |
| 8 | Grief at Work — When You Can't Bring It | Disenfranchised grief; ACT values | The grief you carry into a job that doesn't acknowledge it. Micro-strategies. Saying enough without saying everything. |
| 9 | Anniversary Grief, Late Grief, Stuck Grief | Bonanno; complicated/prolonged grief | When grief shows up on dates, years late, or refuses to move. The clinical line — when to seek a grief therapist. Crisis pointer. |
| 10 | What You Carry Forward — Continuing Bonds | Klass et al.; Cacciatore; cultural traditions | The ongoing relationship with what is lost. Día de los Muertos, shiva, kurova guva. Living *with*, not *past*. |

## 5. Content-care guardrails (mandatory per lesson)

1. **The exit ramp.** Every lesson includes a sentence — usually in the `hook` or `mission` — that explicitly tells the user *they can stop*. Examples: *"If today is too heavy, this is enough — come back when you can."* / *"If reading this is more than you can carry today, close it. The lesson will wait."* The companion is not pushing.

2. **Crisis pointer where relevant.** Lessons 9 and 10 (and any moment that touches suicidal ideation, self-harm, or acute crisis) end with an explicit pointer to the crisis resources Rwendo has loaded for the user's region. Phrasing: *"If you are in crisis or thinking of harming yourself, please use the crisis resources Rwendo loaded for your region — they are listed in the Help screen."* The app reads `jurisdiction.crisisResources` at runtime; we do not hardcode hotline numbers in the lesson copy.

3. **No prescriptive timelines.** Never say *"you should be feeling X by now."* Bonanno's data shows the variance is enormous and the modal trajectory is faster than folklore claims, but plenty of healthy people grieve longer. Both are okay.

4. **No metaphysical claims.** We do not say where the dead are, what death "means," or whether something happens after. The user's framework — religious, secular, agnostic — is theirs. We respect it without endorsing or contradicting.

5. **Worked examples include the harder forms.** Across the ten lessons, examples reference: a parent who never apologised; a divorce that ended badly; a child who walked away; a partner lost to addiction; a stillbirth; an estranged sibling at the funeral; the relief that came alongside the grief; the friend who said the wrong thing. We do not flinch.

6. **Cultural humility.** When we reference Día de los Muertos, shiva, kurova guva, or any other tradition, we name it accurately, attribute it, and do not generalise it into "everyone should." We use these as evidence of the universality of *continuing-bond* practices, not as recommendations.

7. **The companion's tone.** Rwen in this track speaks *less* than usual, *slower* than usual, and *does not analyse*. Phase 8 conversations specify "fewer words than the user; no fixing; no why-questions; mirror and witness." This is the load-bearing tonal choice of the track.

8. **No required mission.** The mission in this track is offered, never demanded. Several lessons explicitly say "skip today's practice if you need to. The reading was the practice."

## 6. Pedagogical fit with the Rwendo Method

The 7-phase flow maps to grief content with a small re-weighting:

- **Hook** carries more weight. It does the work of naming the user's grief without assuming its shape and offering the exit ramp.
- **Chunks** become *named ideas* — *resilience trajectory*, *ambiguous loss*, *continuing bonds*. Naming is most of what this track gives.
- **Pattern** becomes *the underlying claim* — usually a correction of received folklore.
- **Exercises** are gentler. We avoid `multiple_choice` questions that test the user's grief; we favour `match_pairs` (definitional), `fill_blank` (completing a remembered claim), and `translate` prompts that ask the user to articulate, in their own words, what the lesson named for them. `build_sentence` is used to surface the central one-line claim of each lesson.
- **rwenDialogue** is a witnessing conversation, not a teaching one. User-choice options are all marked `correct: true` where they are honest answers — there is no wrong way to be in grief.
- **Active recall** asks for the *name* of the concept, not a felt-sense report. We do not grade the user's grief.
- **Mission** is small, optional, and concrete. Light a candle. Write one sentence. Sit with one feeling for ten seconds. Skip if needed.
- **Phase 8** is the most emotionally weighted of any track in Rwendo. We specify "Rwen speaks less than the user" in `rwenRole` and "the user feels heard" in `successCriteria`, not any cognitive outcome.
